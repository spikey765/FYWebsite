What is Computational Thinking?

This unit aims to introduce students to Computational Thinking: the thought processes involved in formulating a problem and expressing its solution(s) in such a way that a computer—human or machine—can effectively carry it out. This is all about Communication.

How do we communicate our ideas to other people, to machines, or indeed communicate between machines?

Decomposition & Analysis
Decomposition is the process of breaking down a larger problem into smaller, much more manageable ones. Analysing the problem will give insights on how to tackle and decompose

Design & Refine
Design a prototype solution based upon the series of decomposed tasks. Test each solution against general and edge cases then refine at the end of each sprint

Present & Demonstrate
Once a final solution is found and working, the solution must be presented to be understood and carried out by humans or machines via flowcharts or pseudocode alike

Critique & Compare
Scrutinise the final solution to find faulty edge cases or infinite run times. Compare with other solutions and collaborate with peers to find the most optimum solution

What Will I Learn?

  • How to decompose a problem into a series of ordered steps
  • Being able to apply techniques such as decomposition, abstraction, and generalisation to problems
  • How we describe computational complexity in an informal way
  • To explain how self-referential problems relate to the limits of computation

How am I assessed?

At the end of the semester, you will sit an exam worth 50%. During the semester, you will be working with your assigned group to produce solutions to each block. These blocks count as coursework worth 40%. After each block, you will submit a buddy check for you and your team to rate each other based upon their contribution and team work. You will also write individual reflections to discuss how the block went - these evaluations are worth 10%.

You will partake in 6 blocks, each covering different topics previously mentioned. Each block lasts usually a week and you are expected to work with your groups outside of timetabled labs.

Block 0
This block will introduce you to your groups and your lecturers. You will learn the basics of this module and what is expected of you. There will be a live session, videos and some suggested reading for you to complete. This block does not hold any weighting to the final grade.

Block 1
Block 1 is concerned with how to describe an algorithm. You will be given a maze and asked to create an algorithm to solve this maze for a human or computer to follow. This will give an experience of what the rest of the modules are like and is a formative piece of work.

Block 2
We look at further tools that help us create and describe algorithms, such as decomposing problems and algorithms, different kinds of loops, refinement, the idea of generalizing an algorithm that allows it to be applied to a problem and how to test and scrutinize your algorithm and its description.

Block 3
Abstraction is a technique that allows us to strip irrelevant detail from a problem, only retaining what is required to describe a solution. Doing this also allows us to apply the same algorithm to many different problems by realizing that they have a common abstraction. We will make use of graphs.

Block 4
We look at the complexity of both problems and algorithms, and we look at how we can count the number of steps our algorithm is going to take, for both particular examples as well as in general. You will examine example solutions from previous blocks for their complexity.

Block 5
Recursion is a technique for which a program can call a function or subroutine within itself and is a very powerful tool for algorithm design. You will learn to design a recursive algorithm and understand the time complexity that comes with it to find your solution.
